# Write Scientific Grant

Use this skill to make proposals easy for tired reviewers to understand and advocate for.

## Workflow

1. Identify the funder, mechanism, audience, page limits, and review criteria.
2. State the central problem, hypothesis, and deliverable in direct prose.
3. Make the proposal easy for an advocate to defend in a review meeting.
4. Explain why the work fits the funding program.
5. Use short sentences and short paragraphs. Do not rely on underlining, bolding, or typographic rescue.
6. Explain new technology in enough detail that a non-specialist reviewer can follow the logic.
7. Tie preliminary data to feasibility, not just enthusiasm.
8. Make aims independent enough that failure of one aim does not sink the proposal.
9. Name risks and alternatives honestly.
10. End major sections with the impact on the field or problem.

## Specific Aims Pattern

1. Opening paragraph: important problem and current barrier.
2. Knowledge gap: what is unknown and why it matters.
3. Central hypothesis or objective.
4. Aims: each aim has an action verb, testable output, and rationale.
5. Payoff paragraph: expected outcome, impact, and fit to funder.
